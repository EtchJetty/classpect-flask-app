    Let me tell you a story.

    There was a boy in a room, and he was god, but he wasn’t god at all when he woke up, and there was no end.


    I crawl out of bed, moving more out of primal instinct than conscious thought to shut off of the brain-carving sound of my alarm clock. My neck itches with the mess that my hair always transforms into, sometime during the night. I think unflattering thoughts about myself for the allotted time, and then I go to take a shower. The warm water manages to coax me into a state resembling awareness, and I stumble out looking like a somewhat respectable human being, if one in need of a shave.
    (need more than a shave.)

    Tap tap tap, against the side of the sink, and then cold water for the strange sort of sensation that comes with shaving. I look in the mirror, still profoundly unhappy with myself, but at least I wasn’t who I was fifteen minutes ago.

    (maybe the me that lived yesterday died to give way to who I am today. Maybe that’s all life is, living and dying every day, and then your memories get uploaded to the next version of you. You’re a copy of a copy of a copy.)

    I sigh, plop in my earbuds, pick a playlist (some indie creator on youtube, I can’t remember the name,) and then move forwards with my life.

    Making breakfast is always a bit of a lottery. Will it be eggs and bacon? Will I be lazy and make a sandwich? Will I just stare at the fridge and hope food spontaneously makes itself? Find out today, on Will This Loser Get His Fucking Life Together?
    (the answer is no. pick up the knife.)

    Something’s playing on the tv. I don’t remember leaving it on last night, but memories of last night are always a hit or miss concept. Right now it just looks like static and a needle on a record, so I ignore it. Music drifts in between my ears without really registering in my brain. I’m existing alongside the song, not hearing it. Baby, baby, baby, yeah.

    The sound of sizzling meat hits my senses, and it’s bacon, no eggs. I’m too tired to try and crack them— I just know that I’ll make a mess everywhere, and I really can’t afford to burn so many eggs.

    Bacon and toast and… avocado. That’s fine. People have that for breakfast, right? I’m not that weird. Bit a salt...there. I grab my plate and move to the table, but as I swing my body, my chord gets stuck on a knob for a cabinet and they’re subsequently ripped out of my ears.

    Silence screams at me, more assaulting then any noise could be. My skin prickles as I lack distraction from myself, from the room, from the world. The tv whines, hollowly hissing at me as it tries to tell me about the weather, it’s raining cats and dogs and dead dads. I set down the plate at the counter and then grab my earbuds, trying not to feel like I’m doing something wrong. Electricity won’t stop crawling up my spine, like a spider, like a hand—

    Stop.

    Just…

    Stop. (I just want everything to stop so I can breathe for a moment please I just need a minute to breathe and figure out what’s wrong with me.)

    I jam in my music again, and find relief. More lyrics drift in, as I try to think about anything else while eating.

    You don’t want to be…

    Scoop out the flesh of the avocado, cut the bacon, grab the bread. Same as yesterday and the day before that. I pull up a video about… I don’t remember. A game, maybe. It’s not funny, but it’s something. A distraction from the empty house (empty pages. The white light overtakes everything.) I finish up, and the music comes back as I wash dishes. There’s a certain comfort in the mechanical motions. Water, sponge, move your hand, listen to the water rush down the disposal, leave your insides by the door—

    A plate tumbles out of my hand, and old, battered ceramics shatters against the faux-wooden floor, and I don’t do anything but stare at the mess for a while. My fingers feel numb and useless. Eventually, I tiptoe over to the cabinet, grab a broom, sweep up the mess as best I can. The tv’s really screaming now. Screaming bloody murder. Crimson’s leaking out of the speakers, hissing in that same tone that the telephone likes to spit when you try to call someone who doesn’t exist anymore. A sound devoid of meaning— taking up space without actually saying anything. (like me.) The floors are even more of a mess now. I’m gonna have to mop it all up.

    I go back upstairs.

    I miss having other people in the house.


    Click. Click. Click.

    I’m rotating between tabs. Waiting for a new message. For something to happen. For someone to reach out and see me.

    My phone buzzes next to me. I don’t recognize the number, but I pick it up anyway, out of some mix of boredom and longing to hear a voice. I can hear the red swelling up the stairs. It’s gonna swallow me up soon.

    Click.

    “Hello?” I say, instead of something witty or clever. “Smith residence.”

    “Hey.” the girl over the line says. “Are you still there?”

    “Yeah, I just picked up—”

    “No, listen to me. You need to get out. Look at the clock.”

    I glance over at my alarm clock. The face is melting, plastic liquefying into a substance as dark as ink, spilling down into the floor, blue numbers flickering in the midnight oil, like flares launched from the deep. “Don’t you see?”

    I look back at the computer screen. The chatroom is gone now. It’s just an empty page, screaming white, a howling void.

    “Don’t you see?”

    I’m tipping back on my chair. I fall into the blue-stained ink. It swirls around inside my lungs even as I sink deeper, the light of the computer so far away.

    Who am I today is going to die to give way to who I am tomorrow.

    “What happens to the storyteller when he gets lost inside his own creation?”

    The darkness crawls behind my eyes, and touches something inside my brain.

    I crawl out of bed, moving more out of primal instinct than conscious thought to shut off of the brain-carving sound of my alarm clock. I crawl out of bed, moving more out of primal instinct than conscious thought to shut off of the brain-carving sound of my alarm clock.





    “Dear? It’s time to wake up.”

    I open my eyes.

    A middle aged woman smiles at me, eyes tired behind her glasses.

    I still have ink and blood stuck in my throat, static screaming in my head.

    “My name is Doctor Yamada.” She says, kneeling down to meet me on the floor. “Welcome to the Parahuman Asylum in Philadelphia.”

    Let me tell you a story.

    Once there was a boy who sought the face of God, and yet all he ever found was his own reflection.


    They tell me I’m here because I need help getting...better. They never actually explain what that means, though. Am I better when I don’t space out anymore? Am I better when I don’t talk about things that I’m interested in? Am I better when I take my pills and don’t complain about the fact that I feel like I’m missing some core component that the rest of the human race has?

    No one tells me. They just smile (or don’t) and tell me to follow the rules, take my meds, and go to therapy.

    Apparently, I need...fixing.

    I want to know how I got broken in the first place.




    I scream, for a long good while, until red comes up and I can’t scream anymore.

    I go to the phone. There’s a voice on it. She tells me not to listen to the radios. There aren’t any radios here, so I ask her if she can come pick me up. The phone tells me to stay on the line, but then my time is up and I have to go.

    I feel empty in the morning. There’s a name burning on the tip of my tongue.

    I go to the cafeteria, and eat food, and no one speaks to me, because I am the new kid and no one talks to the new kid, because this isn’t that kind of movie.

    I go to therapy. Yamada says that I’m making progress. We’re working on trying to remember my name. Right now they’ve been calling me Mr. Smith. I don’t know what my first name is.

    I wake up.

    I’m leaving the hospital. I go home, and it is soft and warm and there are people there who love me and I feel safe.

    Wait.

    No.

    That’s all wrong.

    Reverse all of that.


    There are other people here. I don’t talk to them much. The TVs are always changing channels, and I can’t understand the garble of weather reports and dial-in orders. Colors bleed out from the NO SIGNAL messages, oozing like blood from a wound, yellow and red in the eye. It gets all over my feet. They don’t allow you to wear shoes in here. Something about laces. We’re all wearing fuzzy sandals. No, that’s not… I don’t remember the name.

    Names.

    I know a lot of names. They buzz around inside my head like fireflies, when I like to pretend that I am somewhere else. Yamada’s stands out like it was written in gasoline and fire, but I can’t recall why.

    I grab a marker and start writing it. Something about discoloring the white wall with black is calming. Breathing life into the nothing.

    I write and write until I have worn it down to the stub.


    I am informed that writing names all over my walls is not an action that will make me better. My marker privileges have been revoked. Instead, they give me paper and some weird kind of pen that I apparently cannot use to hurt myself. (is that a challenge?) I write Yamada’s name, and then Taylor’s. Lisa’s. And Brian and Alec and Rachel and Colin and this is not an oath or a malediction or a curse and—



    But I keep writing.

    I bleed but I keep writing.

    Maybe I could learn to be okay.



    I pass out, and wake up back in my room, names still scrawled all over the walls, and I hurt, but I smile through the blood and ink anyway because I remember.

    Worm.


    I make a list. There are many lists, but this one is mine.

    • I cannot remember who I am.
    • I cannot remember where I am from.
    • I cannot remember why I am here.
    • I am here because I am a parahuman.
    • I am here because I am different.
    • I am here because I am Mr. Smith.


    “Hey. New kid.”

    I look up at a girl. Her TV screen— no, her face— displays trepidation and apprehension. Those are two words that mean the same thing.

    “Hey.” I say back, because I lack wit and charm.

    “I have to meet my quota,” she mutters, sitting next to me with a plate of something that vaguely resembles food. “For talking to other people. And you’re...new.”

    “Thank you?” I say. “I’m… Mr. Smith.”

    “Mimi. Do you have a first name?” Her affect is flat. She's present but not really here. Part of her is stuck somewhere else, and she can't get out. (None of us can. Yesterday becomes tomorrow as fast as today becomes yesterday. It's all the same, in here.)

    I stare down at my plate, hoping to find a sign. “No,” I say. “I don’t remember.”

    “Well, I’m not calling you Mr. Smith.” She stares at me for a while. “Give me a few minutes. I’ll come up with something.”

    “Okay.” I say. We both stare at the table for a bit.

    “Why are you here?” she asks. “I’m not supposed to ask that question.”

    “I don’t know.” I say. “And there’s a lot of things we’re not supposed to do.” It gets a snort out of her, and I smile a little.

    “D’you believe in God?” she asks. The question hits me like a truck, but she keeps on going. “My parents did. Devout Catholics. They went on and on about how you could find Him, so long as you were looking in the right places.” She snorts again, but this one is weighted and bitter. “Look how that worked out for them.”

    “How did it work out for them?”

    Her eyes meet mine, and I feel the ghost of flames licking at my skin. I see a church, old stone wheezing and popping screaming as it’s converted into so much tinder. Stained glass explodes and leaves shattered angels laying the ground. I see a girl, kneeling at the sight of destruction and feeling nothing but emptiness, and deciding to let the fire feel for her.

    “You tell me.” She responds.

    “You looked in the right place.”

    “What?”

    “For God.” I say. “Everyone talks about finding God, but you can find Him anywhere, if you want to. You found God in a gasoline can and a match. I found Him in a blank page and a dream.”

    “...I’m gonna go now.”

    “Okay.” I’m not hurt. I (fucked it up again goddamn it why can’t I not fuck it up why can’t I just be normal) am not hurt.

    “...Adam.” She mutters. “Adam Smith.”

    “Adam.” I say, tasting the name. “...I like it. Thanks.”

    “No problem, new kid.”

    She walks away.

    I have a name.

    I fall asleep feeling a little less empty.
